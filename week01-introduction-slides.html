Discussion

Lecture and readings

  • Government = “the whole body of rules, practices and institutions under whose guidance we live together in societies”
  • Political theory is concerned not only with different systems of government but also with the relationship and tension between different values within one system (e.g. liberty vs. equality vs. community)
  • Political theory ≠ social and political science — BUT “political philosophers…need to have a good grasp of social and political science”
  • “The agenda of political philosophy changes as society and government change”

Discussion

Discussion questions

  1. Miller argues that political philosophy matters because governments profoundly affect our lives. Can you think of a recent example where a government decision directly shaped how people live?
  2. Miller distinguishes political philosophy from political science. What do you think political theory can tell us that empirical research alone cannot?
  3. O'Sullivan challenges liberal political theory for marginalising Indigenous perspectives. What are the risks of building political theories primarily from one cultural tradition?
  4. O'Sullivan calls for a political theory that goes "beyond the colonial." What might that look like in practice — in Australia or elsewhere?

Activity 1

Art and political theory

In this week's reading, Miller (2003) illustrates the importance of political theory by analysing Ambrogio Lorenzetti's Allegory of Good and Bad Government.

Task: In groups, identify a work of art that illustrates one or more of these three aspects of political theory:

  1. The impact that government (good and bad) can have on people's lives
  2. That people can choose (in most cases) what their government should look like
  3. That we can make a distinction between good and bad government

10:00

Activity 1

Share your art selection

Share with the class the work of art you selected and why, walking through how it demonstrates the three key aspects.

10:00

Activity 2

Essay question brainstorm

In the same groups, consider one of the below questions set for the Essay assessment. Discuss how you would respond to it. Arrive at a consensus and brainstorm at least two supporting points.

  1. What limits should be placed on who is allowed to speak on university campuses?
  2. Is incivility ever justified? Under what conditions?
  3. Are compulsory voting systems better than voluntary ones?
  4. Is it a problem if voters choose candidates based on how they speak?
  5. Should restrictions be placed on the extent to which people from minoritised religions are able to shape public space?

Activity 2
