Discussion

Readings discussion

In your groups, pick two of these questions to discuss. Be ready to share your position.

  1. Barendt: Why should speech receive special protection rather than just ordinary liberty protection? Is there a good answer?
  2. Barendt: Is there a single best justification for free speech—truth, autonomy, or democracy—or do we need all three?
  3. Simpson: Are universities primarily knowledge-producing institutions or democratic public forums?
  4. Simpson: Who gets to decide what counts as intellectually serious speech on campus?
  5. Both: Can a university protect open inquiry without also protecting broad free speech?

Campus speakers

Activity 2

Counterspeech

Some scholars have defended the idea of counterspeech (e.g. responding to others' speech through marches and demonstrations, or on social media) as an alternative to the legal regulation of hate speech, as it allows "victims of hate speech or other citizens speaking back to hate-mongers … to assert their rights and equal civic status" (Bonotti and Seglow 2021: 5).

In line with this view, former president of the ACLU, Nadine Strossen, explains why she finds counterspeech preferable to legal restrictions.

5:00

Activity 2

Counterspeech campaign

In groups, take on the role of a civil society organisation (e.g. a community-based organisation, women's rights group, or faith-based organisation) that is planning a counterspeech campaign on behalf of victims of hate speech linked to the group you represent. Develop a plan that:

  • Identifies the hate-monger(s) your counterspeech campaign will target
  • Involves a specific, well thought through counterspeech activity (e.g. a demonstration, march, flyer/poster, or social media post)
  • Includes contingencies for any potential issues/setbacks you might face

15:00

Activity 2

Share your plan

Share your counterspeech campaign plan with the rest of the class.
