Discussion

Lecture and readings

Lecture outline:

  1. A civility crisis? — Trump, Thorpe swearing in, Inhofe snowball
  2. What is civility? — Three dimensions: politeness, moral civility, justificatory civility (Bardon et al. 2023); politeness norms are contextual and can change (e.g. COVID-19)
  3. A civility crisis (redux)? — Classifying the examples: moral incivility, incivility as impoliteness, justificatory incivility
  4. The means and ends of civility — Dimensions co-exist and interact; deep vs surface-level politeness/civility; critical impoliteness/incivility
  5. A civility crisis (re-redux)? — Reclassifying: deep impoliteness, critical impoliteness, deep incivility
  6. Incivility as dissent — Incivility can have positive democratic value; power asymmetries matter

Discussion

Readings discussion

In your groups, pick two of these questions to discuss. Be ready to share your position.

  1. Bardon et al.: In Week 2 you debated campus speakers like Pauline Hanson and Thomas Sewell. Using Bardon et al.'s three dimensions, were those speakers polite, morally civil, and justificatorily civil? Can someone score high on one dimension but low on another?
  2. Bardon et al.: Many of you are interested in global politics and armed conflict. Can a political leader use polite, diplomatic language while advocating policies that undermine the equal standing of certain groups? What does this “surface-level politeness” tell us about the limits of politeness as a measure of civility?
  3. Edyvane: Several of you flagged gender, equality, and rights as important topics. Edyvane argues incivility can be a form of dissent. When marginalised groups use impolite tactics — protests, disruptions, confrontational language — is this justified? Where would you draw the line?
  4. Both: In Week 2 we explored counterspeech as an alternative to regulating hate speech. Is counterspeech always civil? Could effective counterspeech sometimes require incivility? How would Bardon et al. classify a passionate counter-protest?
  5. Both: Think of a recent controversy you care about — climate activism, protests over the Middle East, or the rise of the far right. Classify the actors using Bardon et al.'s means-and-ends framework. Is anyone engaging in what Edyvane would call “incivility as dissent”?

Activity 1

Incivility as dissent

Edyvane (2020) argues that incivility can have a positive democratic value in contemporary liberal democracies, and that incivility can be a form of dissent employed, for example, by members of minoritised groups.

Task: Drawing on this week's readings, choose an example of incivility as dissent which has been or might be employed by each group below, and why. Discuss whether this instance of incivility is acceptable.
