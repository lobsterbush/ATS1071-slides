Model

The calculus of voting

Why do people vote when their single vote almost never decides an election? Riker & Ordeshook (1968) formalised this as:

R = pB − C + D

  • p — probability your vote is decisive (vanishingly small in large electorates)
  • B — benefit from your preferred candidate winning
  • C — cost of voting (time, effort, information)
  • D — the “duty” term: civic satisfaction, affirming the system, expressing a preference

The paradox: Since p ≈ 0, the instrumental benefit pB ≈ 0. Without D, nobody rational should vote. The D term is what “saves” the rational voter model.

Critique

What D means for compulsory voting

The D term creates a sharp dilemma for CV advocates:

  • If people already have high D: They vote voluntarily — CV is unnecessary
  • If people have low D: CV forces them to the polls, but doesn’t create genuine civic engagement — it just raises the cost of not voting (fines replace C with a penalty)
  • Quality concern: Voters brought in by CV tend to have low D — they are less informed, less partisan, and more likely to cast invalid ballots or vote randomly
  • Expressive value lost: When D motivates voluntary voting, the act means something — a signal of civic commitment. CV removes this signal by making everyone vote regardless of D

Key question: Does CV build civic duty over time (increasing D), or does it just bypass it? This is the empirical question the rest of the evidence tries to answer.

Think about a group whose voting participation has historically been suppressed — e.g. women in newly democratic states or Indigenous Australians before 1962. Do the egalitarian and republican arguments reinforce each other for these groups, or are they in tension?

Critique

The puzzle: How did CV pass?

If CV shifts the median voter away from elite preferences, why didn’t elites block it?

  • Quiet adoption: Belgium (1893), Australia (1924) — often passed as minor procedural reforms, not landmark legislative battles
  • Divided elites: Commercial vs. landed, urban vs. rural — different factions had different calculations about who CV would activate
  • Incumbency advantage: Existing elites believed clientelistic networks and organisational advantages would mobilise their voters more than opposition voters
  • Civic framing: CV was sold as “democratic duty,” not redistribution — obscuring distributional consequences behind good-government language

Key insight: Elites didn’t uniformly lose. The threat was less decisive than median-voter theory predicts, because consequences were uncertain and elites retained influence over what new voters wanted.

Critique

Why the shift may not materialise

Even after CV passes, the expected leftward policy shift can be smaller than proponents assume.

  • Preference formation: Voters don’t arrive with fixed preferences — elites shape what new voters want through media, framing, and party cues
  • Invalid ballots: CV generates higher turnout and higher rates of spoiled ballots, concentrated among poorer voters — transforming unequal voice through low turnout into unequal voice through invalid votes
  • Pre-existing turnout matters: Going from 50% → 90% changes the electorate fundamentally; going from 80% → 90% brings in marginal voters who look much like the average voter
  • Polarisation risk: De Leon & Rizzi (2016) find CV in Brazil made voters more likely to identify with ideological extremes — the opposite of the centrist convergence proponents expect

Discussion: Does this undermine the egalitarian and republican arguments for CV, or does it mean they need complementary reforms?

Evidence

The Austrian evidence

Hoffman, León & Lombardi. 2017. “Compulsory Voting, Turnout, and Government Spending: Evidence from Austria.” Journal of Public Economics 145: 103–115.

doi.org/10.1016/j.jpubeco.2016.10.002

  • Design: Austrian states changed CV laws at different times (1949–2010), providing a natural experiment
  • Turnout effect: CV increased turnout by roughly 10 percentage points (from ~80% to ~90%)
  • Policy effect: No change in government spending — neither level nor composition (welfare, infrastructure, administration)
  • Mechanism: Voters swayed by CV were non-partisan, had low political interest, and were uninformed — their votes didn’t shift electoral outcomes

Takeaway: More voters ≠ different policy. The case for CV may need to rest on intrinsic democratic values, not on expected policy change.
