Lecture and readings

Lecture outline:

  1. What is linguistic justice? — Managing different language groups within a political community (De Schutter & Robichaud 2015). Two approaches: equal treatment of all languages vs. one shared lingua franca
  2. Three goals of language policy: Identity interests (autonomy, dignity), non-identity interests (efficiency, democracy, equality of opportunity), and the intrinsic value of languages
  3. Linguistic prejudice & deliberative democracy: Intra-linguistic prejudice (within a shared language) undermines democratic deliberation — what about aural expressions, not just visual ones? (Elster 2013)
  4. Linguistic prejudice & electoral discrimination: Rehfeld’s voter-exogenous vs. voter-endogenous constraints — and why Bonotti & Willoughby (2023) argue his framework is too permissive
  5. Sociolinguistics evidence: Matched guise tests — people consistently judge speakers by accent; ethnically-marked varieties rated as less competent
  6. Australian examples: Gladys Liu, Mathias Cormann, Jacqui Lambie, Julia Gillard, Kristina Keneally

Key concept

Constraints on candidates

Rehfeld (2010) distinguishes two types of constraints on who gets elected:

Voter-exogenous

  • External rules and structures
  • Quotas, secured seats, gerrymandering, funding rules
  • Rehfeld considers these undemocratic

Voter-endogenous

  • Emerge from voter judgment
  • Voters prefer some candidates over others
  • Rehfeld considers these democratic

The problem: Bonotti & Willoughby (2023) argue that not all voter-endogenous constraints are acceptable. Incompetent and biased voter judgment — including that driven by linguistic prejudice — undermines candidates’ equal right to run for office.

Evidence

Hierarchies of linguistic prejudice

Not all non-standard accents are penalised equally. The lecture highlighted a key asymmetry:

  • European accents (e.g. Cormann’s German accent) — pass unremarked or coded as favourably cosmopolitan (Ramjattan 2019)
  • Racialised accents (e.g. Gladys Liu’s Chinese accent) — heard as problematic, feed into narratives of “not one of us”
  • Class-marked accents (e.g. Jacqui Lambie’s broad Australian) — signal low education, lack of sophistication
  • Gender + accent (e.g. Julia Gillard vs. Bob Hawke) — women judged more harshly for the same non-standard forms (Bayard et al. 2001)

The “damned if you do” problem: Keneally was criticised for sounding “too American,” then criticised again for allegedly undergoing voice coaching to sound more Australian. Non-standard speakers face impossible trade-offs.

Theory

Understanding discrimination

The lecture showed that voters discriminate against candidates based on how they speak. But why does this happen? Economists and political scientists distinguish two fundamental mechanisms:

Taste-based  ↔  Statistical

  • Taste-based discrimination (Becker 1957) — pure animus: the discriminator has a “taste” for avoiding a group regardless of individual characteristics
  • Statistical discrimination (Arrow 1973; Phelps 1972) — using group membership as a signal for unobserved individual traits (e.g. competence, reliability)

Why does this matter? Both types produce unequal outcomes, but the causes — and therefore the remedies — are different. Understanding which mechanism is at work shapes what we can do about it.

Theory

Two models of discrimination

Taste-based (Becker)

  • Mechanism: Decision-makers derive disutility from interacting with members of a group — they pay a cost to discriminate
  • Example: A voter refuses to support a candidate with a foreign accent, even knowing the candidate is highly qualified
  • Persistence: Survives even when full information is available
  • Remedy: Anti-discrimination laws, social norms change, contact

Statistical (Arrow/Phelps)

  • Mechanism: Decision-makers use group identity to infer hard-to-observe traits — it’s a “rational” shortcut under uncertainty
  • Example: A voter hears a regional accent and infers (incorrectly) that the speaker is less educated
  • Persistence: Can be reduced by providing more individual-level information
  • Remedy: Better information, credential signals, blind evaluation

Key point: In practice, the two mechanisms often co-exist and reinforce each other. Accent triggers both statistical inferences (“she must be less educated”) and taste-based reactions (“that accent annoys me”).

Application

Discrimination & linguistic prejudice

The matched guise tests from the lecture (Lambert et al. 1960) reveal both mechanisms at work:

  • Statistical: Listeners hear a non-standard accent → infer lower education, social status, or competence. The accent acts as a signal for unobserved traits. This is why socially prestigious accents are rated higher on competence (Garrett 2010).
  • Taste-based: Some accent prejudice persists even when listeners know the speaker’s actual qualifications. The prejudice is formed early in life and is “particularly resistant to change” (Garrett 2001) — suggesting an affective component beyond rational inference.
  • Intersectional: The hierarchies of prejudice (Ramjattan 2019) — European accents favoured, racialised accents penalised — suggest that taste-based prejudice against marginalised groups amplifies statistical discrimination.

The insidiousness: Bonotti & Willoughby’s core argument is that linguistic prejudice is particularly dangerous because people don’t recognise it as prejudice. Unlike racial or gender bias, accent bias flies under the radar of democratic accountability.

Research spotlight

Measuring discrimination: Audit experiments

How do we actually measure discrimination? One powerful method — directly relevant to the matched guise tests in this week’s lecture — is the audit (correspondence) experiment.

  • The logic: Send identical messages/applications that vary only on one characteristic (name, accent, appearance) and measure differential responses
  • Names as signals: Racially distinctive names signal both race and class (Crabtree, Gaddis et al. 2022, Sociological Science) — names function as audible cues in the same way accents do
  • Email discrimination: Americans are less likely to reply to emails from Black people relative to White people (Block, Crabtree, Holbein & Monson 2021, PNAS)
  • Institutional gatekeepers: School principals discriminate against Muslim and atheist families (Pfaff, Crabtree, Kern & Holbein 2021, PAR); local election officials show persistent bias (Hughes, Crabtree et al. 2020, JEPS)

Method connection: Audit experiments work exactly like the matched guise tests from the lecture — hold everything constant except the signal of group identity, and measure how responses change.

Application

Implications: Intersectionality & policy

Discrimination rarely operates along a single axis, and the mechanism matters for remedies:

  • Intersectional penalties compound: A meta-analysis finds the penalty for belonging to two marginalised groups is greater than the sum of each separately (Gaddis, Crabtree et al., forthcoming, Sociological Science). A female candidate from a migrant background with a non-standard accent faces compounding disadvantages.
  • If mostly statistical: More information about candidates should reduce bias — voters who learn actual qualifications should discount accent
  • If mostly taste-based: More information won’t help — this matches Garrett’s (2001) finding that linguistic attitudes form early and resist education. Bonotti & Willoughby’s case for treating it as an undemocratic constraint is strongest here.

Discussion: Think about the Australian examples from the lecture. Which mechanism — statistical or taste-based — best explains the treatment of Gladys Liu? Of Jacqui Lambie? Do the mechanisms differ across cases?
